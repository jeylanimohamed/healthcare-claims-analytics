# Healthcare Claims Analytics

**Mohamed Jeylani** | July 2026

I wanted to build something that looked like the work I actually did at my internships — claims data, cost analysis, figuring out which patients are most at risk. So I generated 25,000 synthetic Medicare-style claims across 5,000 patients and 200 providers and ran the numbers.

---

## What I Did

- Generated realistic claims data with patient demographics, diagnosis codes, procedure costs, and admission flags
- Wrote 7 SQL queries to answer questions about cost drivers, regional variation, and readmission rates
- Ran exploratory analysis in pandas, matplotlib, and seaborn
- Trained a Random Forest model to predict admission risk (ROC-AUC: 0.72 — not amazing, but it works)

---

## Dataset

| Table | Records | Description |
|---|---|---|
| `patients` | 5,000 | Demographics, chronic conditions, risk scores |
| `providers` | 200 | Specialty, region, patient panel size |
| `claims` | 25,000 | Diagnosis codes, procedure costs, admission flags, length of stay |

The data is synthetic (generated by `data/generate_data.py`), but I tried to make it follow patterns you'd see in real CMS claims.

---

## What I Found

1. **High-risk patients cost 1.6× more** than low-risk ($621 vs $390 per patient). Makes sense — but seeing the dollar gap was still useful.

2. **67.3% of admitted patients** get readmitted within the year. I knew readmissions were a big deal in healthcare, but I didn't expect the number to be that high.

3. **Age dominates admission risk** (67% feature importance), with risk score a distant second at 16%. I thought chronic conditions would matter more — turns out age is just the strongest signal by a lot.

4. **38.7% of claims** result in hospital admission, driving 41.5% of total costs. A small slice of claims eats up a disproportionate share of spending.
